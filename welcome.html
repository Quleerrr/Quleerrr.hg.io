Local culture

New Zealand's local culture is a rich tapestry woven from its indigenous Maori heritage, European influences, and diverse immigrant communities.

The Maori are New Zealand's indigenous Polynesian population, with a rich cultural legacy. Their language, Te Reo Maori, is an official language in New Zealand, alongside English. Maori culture pervades many parts of New Zealand life, including art, music, dance (such as the haka), and cuisine.

VIEW MORE INFORMATION ABOUT MAORI CULTURE

  • Gender

    Traditional Maori society had defined roles for men and women, with women often taking on responsibilities related to family and domestic affairs, while men were involved in hunting, warfare, and leadership. However, Maori culture also has a strong tradition of respecting women and their contributions. In contemporary Maori society, there is a growing emphasis on gender equality and the recognition of women's rights.

  • Class

    Historically, Maori society had a hierarchical structure based on tribal affiliations and chiefly status. However, with colonization and subsequent socio-economic changes, traditional class distinctions have become less prominent. Today, there are still disparities in socio-economic status among Maori communities, but efforts are being made to address these through initiatives promoting education, employment, and economic development.

  • Religion

    Traditional Maori religion, known as Te Ao Mārama (The World of Light), involves a complex system of spiritual beliefs and practices centered around the worship of ancestors, nature, and deities. With the arrival of European settlers, Christianity was introduced to Maori communities and has since become widely practiced alongside traditional beliefs. Maori spirituality continues to play a significant role in cultural ceremonies and rituals.

  • Ethnicity

    Maori culture places a strong emphasis on kinship, community, and belonging. Identity and ethnicity are closely tied to ancestry and whakapapa (genealogy) within Maori society.

Local Life

New Zealand's local life reflects the country's diverse cultural influences and stunning natural surroundings. From bustling cities to quaint rural towns, life in New Zealand offers a unique blend of modernity and tranquility.

One of the defining aspects of local life is the strong connection to nature. Kiwis, as New Zealanders are affectionately known, often embrace outdoor activities such as hiking, camping, and surfing. With an abundance of national parks, beaches, and scenic landscapes, there's no shortage of opportunities to explore the great outdoors.

Furthermore, New Zealanders take pride in their laid-back and friendly demeanor. Communities are tight-knit, and a sense of camaraderie prevails in both urban and rural settings. This welcoming atmosphere extends to visitors, making it easy for newcomers to feel at home.

In terms of cuisine, New Zealand offers a diverse culinary scene influenced by its multicultural population. From traditional Maori hangi feasts to innovative fusion dishes, there's something to satisfy every palate.

Overall, life in New Zealand is characterized by its beauty, warmth, and sense of adventure. Whether enjoying a leisurely weekend barbecue with friends or embarking on a spontaneous road trip, residents cherish the simple pleasures and natural wonders that define their homeland.

National Heros

Kate Sheppard

A leading figure in the women's suffrage movement, she played an important role in making New Zealand the first country to grant women the right to vote in 1893. Her great campaigning and leadership inspired suffragist movements worldwide and left a lasting impact on gender equality in New Zealand and nearby regions.

Edmund Hillary

An iconic mountaineer and explorer, Sir Edmund Hillary, alongside Tenzing Norgay, has became one of the first climbers to reach the summit of Mount Everest in 1953. Beyond mountaineering, Hillary was also a philanthropist, who contributed significantly to the welfare of the Sherpa people in Nepal.

New Zealand has a long and diverse tradition in arts and literature in reflection to its diverse cultural heritage. In literature, despite many notable figures, several writers stand out. One of them is Patricia Grace, she is renowned for her novels and short stories that delve into Māori culture and identity, with notable works like "Potiki" and "The Dream Sleepers" . Another notable writer would be Witi Ihimaera, who’s the author of "The Whale Rider," has significantly contributed to bringing Māori stories to a global audience. Finally, one prize-winning author would be Keri Hulme. Her work “The Bone People” won the Booker Prize in 1985,which is another significant work that explores Māori mythology and cultural identity.

Local and Traditional Food You Cannot Refuse

Hāngī - food cooked under the ground

The Māori hāngī is a traditional earth oven style of cooking. Now saved mainly for special occasions, foods cooked in a hāngī include chicken, pork and mutton, as well as pumpkin, potato and kumara (sweet potato). One of the best places to enjoy this cultural feast is Rotorua, in the central North Island, where you can also taste food that has been steam-cooked in naturally heated geothermal pools. You might also get to savour a delicious hāngī-steamed pudding for dessert.

Kiwi summer BBQ

Call it a ‘barbie’, barbeque, barbecue or BBQ – the act of cooking outside is an essential part of Kiwi culture. Lamb, beef, sausages and poultry are usually barbecued at home in the backyard during the summer months. Potato salad, garlic bread and fresh vegetable salads are also prepared. Then, family and friends come together to enjoy the the meal together outside on the deck. To experience a Kiwi BBQ in style, take the Earnslaw cruise to Walter Peak Gourmet BBQ in Queenstown.

Talking with the Local

Verbal Communication

Directness: New Zealanders tend to value honesty and direct communication. They appreciate straightforwardness and may find beating around the bush or indirect communication styles frustrating.

Politeness: While directness is valued, politeness is also important. Using "please," "thank you," and "sorry" appropriately demonstrates good manners and respect.

Sense of Humor: New Zealanders often use humor in their communication. They appreciate wit and sarcasm but may also employ self-deprecating humor. It's important to be able to understand and respond appropriately to humor.

Non-verbal Communication

Personal Space: New Zealanders generally value personal space and may feel uncomfortable with close physical proximity during conversations. Maintain a comfortable distance, especially with acquaintances or strangers.

Facial Expressions: Facial expressions play a significant role in communication. Smiling is generally appreciated and can help to establish rapport. Frowning or displaying negative facial expressions may indicate disagreement or displeasure.

In New Zealand, public displays of affection are usually fine, but it's best to keep them moderate. Holding hands or a quick kiss is generally okay, but anything more intimate might attract unwanted attention or make others uncomfortable.

As for emotions like anger or frustration, it's better to keep them in check when you're out in public. New Zealanders prefer a calm and composed atmosphere, so getting visibly upset or angry in public isn't really the norm. It's more respectful to deal with strong emotions in private or in a controlled environment.

Basically, it's all about being considerate of others and mindful of the social norms. Being respectful of people's space and feelings goes a long way in New Zealand.

Working in New Zealand

Agriculture plays a significant role in New Zealand's economy. The country is known for its agricultural exports, particularly dairy, meat, wool, and horticulture. Dairy farming is a major contributor, with New Zealand being one of the world's largest dairy exporters. Other notable agricultural activities include sheep farming, beef production, and fruit cultivation, especially kiwifruit and wine grapes.

According to Statistics New Zealand, agriculture contributed 7.6% to New Zealand's gross domestic product (GDP) in 2020.

New Zealand's industrial sector encompasses manufacturing, construction, mining, and energy production. While not as dominant as agriculture, industry still plays a significant role in the economy. Manufacturing includes food processing, wood and paper products, machinery, and transportation equipment. Construction is driven by residential and commercial building projects, infrastructure development, and renovation activities.

Manufacturing contributed around 10% to New Zealand's GDP in 2020.

The service sector is the largest contributor to New Zealand's economy, encompassing a wide range of industries such as finance, tourism, education, healthcare, information technology, and professional services. Tourism, in particular, is a significant sector, attracting millions of visitors each year to explore the country's natural beauty, adventure activities, and cultural experiences.

Services accounted for approximately 67% of New Zealand's GDP in 2020.

About New Zealand Sports:

New Zealanders have a deep passion for sports, with a rich history of success in various disciplines. From the iconic All Blacks rugby team to the formidable Black Caps cricket squad, sports play a significant role in the country's culture and identity.

  2. Cricket: Cricket enjoys widespread popularity, especially during the summer months, with the national team, the Black Caps, competing in international tournaments.

Overview

The education system in New Zealand is known for its high quality and emphasis on providing equal opportunities for all students. It comprises several levels, including primary, secondary, tertiary, and vocational education.

Primary & Secondary Education

Primary education in New Zealand typically starts at age 5 and continues until age 12. Secondary education covers students from age 13 to 18 and includes both lower secondary (years 9-10) and upper secondary (years 11-13) levels. Education is compulsory for children aged 6 to 16.

Tertiary Education

Tertiary education in New Zealand includes universities, colleges, and institutes of technology and polytechnics. There are eight government-funded universities offering a wide range of undergraduate and postgraduate programs.

Vocational Education

Vocational education and training (VET) provide practical skills and knowledge for specific industries and occupations. It includes apprenticeships, trade qualifications, and certificate and diploma programs offered by vocational education providers.

About Language

In New Zealand, English is the most commonly spoken language, although it is not legally designated as an official language. It is used by approximately 95.4% of the population. Te reo Māori, spoken by about 4% of the population, and New Zealand Sign Language (NZSL), used by 0.5%, are the two official languages recognized by law. Other common languages used include Samoan, Mandarin, Hindi.etc reflection to the country's diverse immigrant population New Zealand boasts a high literacy rate of approximately 99%.
